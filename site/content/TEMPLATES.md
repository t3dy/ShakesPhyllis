# Writing Templates — Shakespeare Sonnets Site

Generated by `/write-dominic-template`. These templates define what content appears on each page type and how the database fields map to prose output. Used by `build_site.py` and by Claude when generating enrichment data.

---

## TEMPLATE 1: Sonnet Analysis Page

**Format:** Scholarly close-reading with dramatic focus
**Audience:** Educated general reader; Shakespeare enthusiasts; students; actors preparing readings
**Total target:** 800-1,200 words per sonnet
**Tone:** Authoritative but accessible. Vendler-level insight, Fuller-level performance awareness. No jargon without explanation.

### SECTION 1: Overview Banner
**Purpose:** Orient the reader in 3 sentences.
**Word target:** 40-60 words
**Required elements:** Sonnet number, first line (quoted), addressee, sequence group, one-sentence dramatic situation.
**Data source:** `sonnets.number`, `sonnets.first_line`, `sonnets.addressee`, `sonnet_groups.name`, `sonnet_analyses.argument_summary`
**Template:**
```
Sonnet [NUMBER] ("[FIRST_LINE]") is addressed to the [ADDRESSEE] and belongs to the [GROUP_NAME] sequence. [ARGUMENT_SUMMARY].
```
**Anti-patterns:** Don't open with "This sonnet is about..." — lead with the dramatic situation.

---

### SECTION 2: The Sonnet Text (Left Panel)
**Purpose:** Present the poem with structural apparatus.
**Required elements:**
- Full text with line numbers (1-14)
- Stanza labels: Q1 (lines 1-4), Q2 (5-8), Q3 (9-12), Couplet (13-14)
- Volta marker at `sonnet_analyses.volta_line`
- Rhetorical device highlights from `line_devices` (show device name on hover)
**Data source:** `lines.line_text`, `stanzas.stanza_type`, `sonnet_analyses.volta_line`, `line_devices.*`
**Anti-patterns:** Never paraphrase the sonnet instead of showing it. The text is the center; everything else is apparatus.

---

### SECTION 3: Primary Analysis
**Purpose:** The main close reading, organized around the highest-priority analytical mode.
**Word target:** 300-500 words
**Required elements:**
- Lead with the priority-1 `sonnet_modes.mode_id` and its rationale
- Verbatim line citations (minimum 3, with line numbers)
- Volta identification: where it falls, what type, what changes
- Couplet function: how it resolves (or doesn't) the argument
- At least 2 rhetorical devices explained in context (from `line_devices`)
**Data source:** `sonnet_analyses.analysis_text`, `sonnet_modes` (ORDER BY priority DESC), `line_devices.*`
**Template:**
```
[MODE_NAME] is the essential lens for Sonnet [NUMBER]. [RATIONALE].

[ANALYSIS_TEXT — the core reading, with verbatim citations in quotation marks followed by (line N)]

The volta at line [VOLTA_LINE] is [VOLTA_TYPE]: [explanation of what shifts]. The couplet [COUPLET_FUNCTION]s: [explanation].
```
**Anti-patterns:**
- Don't list devices without explaining what they DO in context. "Line 4 contains a metaphor" is useless. "The metaphor in line 4 transforms the FYM from beloved into debtor" is useful.
- Don't treat all 14 lines equally. Lead with what matters most. Some lines are pivotal; some are connective tissue.
- Don't explain what a sonnet form is. The reader knows.

---

### SECTION 4: FYM/DL Dynamics
**Purpose:** Situate the sonnet in the love triangle drama.
**Word target:** 100-200 words
**Required elements:**
- Who appears: from `character_appearances` (role + evidence)
- How this sonnet advances the FYM/DL/Speaker dramatic situation
- Connection to adjacent sonnets in the sequence
**Data source:** `character_appearances.*`, `sonnet_analyses.dramatic_situation`, `sonnet_analyses.subtext`
**Template:**
```
**Characters present:** [CHARACTER_NAME] ([ROLE]: "[EVIDENCE]")

[DRAMATIC_SITUATION]. [SUBTEXT — what the Speaker means but doesn't say].

**In the sequence:** This sonnet follows [N-1] and precedes [N+1]. [Brief note on narrative continuity or shift].
```
**Optional elements:** Historical candidate connections (e.g., "If the FYM is Southampton, then...")
**Anti-patterns:** Don't assume the reader already knows the triangle. Each page should make the dramatic situation self-contained.

---

### SECTION 5: Close Reading by Stanza
**Purpose:** Section-by-section breakdown: Q1, Q2, Q3, Couplet.
**Word target:** 200-300 words
**Required elements:**
- One paragraph per stanza
- What each stanza DOES argumentatively (not just what it says)
- Key device per stanza (from `line_devices`)
- How the stanzas relate to each other (parallel, progressive, antithetical)
**Data source:** `stanzas.*`, `line_devices.*` (filtered by stanza), `line_annotations.commentary`
**Template:**
```
**Q1 (lines [START]-[END]):** [What Q1 does — establishes, proposes, describes]. The key figure is [DEVICE]: "[QUOTATION]" — [EXPLANATION].

**Q2 (lines [START]-[END]):** [How Q2 develops, complicates, or turns from Q1]. [DEVICE + explanation].

**Q3 (lines [START]-[END]):** [How Q3 responds to Q1-Q2 — escalation, reversal, new angle]. [DEVICE + explanation].

**Couplet (lines [START]-[END]):** [How the couplet resolves, reverses, or leaves open the argument]. "[QUOTED COUPLET]" — [EXPLANATION of final effect].
```

---

### SECTION 6: Glossary Sidebar
**Purpose:** Define archaic/specialized terms.
**Required elements:** Term, definition, line reference.
**Data source:** `line_annotations.gloss` (for lines where gloss differs significantly from modern reading)
**Template:**
```
**[TERM]** (line [N]): [DEFINITION]. [Brief note on why it matters here].
```
**Anti-patterns:** Don't gloss obvious words. Only gloss terms where the Elizabethan meaning differs materially from the modern one, or where a pun is active.

## TEMPLATE 2: Sonnet Directing Page

**Format:** Line-by-line performance guide for a dramatic reading
**Audience:** Actors, directors, audiobook performers, poetry readers. Also scholars interested in the sonnets as dramatic utterances.
**Total target:** 600-900 words per sonnet
**Tone:** Direct, practical, dramatic. Like a director's notes in rehearsal: imperative mood, specific, actionable.

### SECTION 1: Performance Frame
**Purpose:** Set up the dramatic context before the line-by-line notes.
**Word target:** 100-150 words
**Required elements:**
- Scene setting (where/when this is being spoken)
- Who the Speaker is in this moment (from `directing_notes.character_note`)
- Who is being addressed (FYM, DL, self, world)
- The emotional arc across the whole sonnet
**Data source:** `directing_notes.scene_setting`, `directing_notes.character_note`, `directing_notes.fym_note`, `directing_notes.dl_note`, `directing_notes.overall_arc`
**Template:**
```
**Scene:** [SCENE_SETTING]

**The Speaker:** [CHARACTER_NOTE]

**The Fair Young Man:** [FYM_NOTE]
[OR]
**The Dark Lady:** [DL_NOTE]

**Arc:** [OVERALL_ARC]
```

---

### SECTION 2: Line-by-Line Table
**Purpose:** The core directing content — one row per line.
**Required elements per row:**
- Line number and stanza label
- Full line text
- Emphasis words (bolded in text)
- Emotional note (what the actor should feel)
- Delivery note (how to say it — pace, volume, tone, pause)
**Data source:** `lines.line_text`, `line_annotations.emphasis_words`, `line_annotations.emotional_note`, `line_annotations.delivery_note`
**Template:**
```
| Line | Stanza | Text | Emotion | Delivery |
|------|--------|------|---------|----------|
| 1 | Q1 | "From **fairest** creatures we **desire** **increase**," | Warm, expansive | Measured pace, open tone. |
```
**Anti-patterns:**
- Don't write generic notes: "Read with feeling" is useless. "Stress 'thou' — this is the volta; the universal becomes personal" is useful.
- Don't give the same note for every line. Each line should have something specific.
- Don't ignore the line breaks. Enjambment and end-stops are performance decisions.

---

### SECTION 3: Key Moments
**Purpose:** Flag the 3-5 most important performance decisions.
**Word target:** 100-150 words
**Required elements:** Line number, specific direction, WHY it matters.
**Data source:** `directing_notes.key_moments` (JSON array of {line, note})
**Template:**
```
**Line [N]:** [NOTE]. [WHY this moment is pivotal for the sonnet's dramatic effect.]
```

---

### SECTION 4: The Volta
**Purpose:** Special attention to the sonnet's turn — THE most important performance moment.
**Word target:** 50-80 words
**Required elements:** Which line, what type of turn, how to perform it (pause? tone shift? volume change? physical gesture?)
**Data source:** `sonnet_analyses.volta_line`, `sonnet_analyses.volta_type`
**Template:**
```
**The turn** arrives at line [VOLTA_LINE] ([VOLTA_TYPE]). [Specific performance direction: how long to pause before the turn, what changes in voice/body/pace, what the listener should feel shift.]
```

## TEMPLATE 3: Character/Candidate Page

**Format:** Scholarly profile of a historical candidate for FYM or DL
**Audience:** General readers and Shakespeare enthusiasts curious about the real people behind the sonnets
**Total target:** 500-800 words per candidate
**Tone:** Evenhanded, evidence-driven. Present the case without overselling it.

### SECTION 1: Identity Card
**Purpose:** Quick-reference identification.
**Required elements:**
- Candidate name and dates
- Which character they're a candidate for (FYM/DL/RP)
- Strength of identification (STRONG/MODERATE/SPECULATIVE)
- Key scholars who argue for this identification
**Data source:** `character_candidates.*`, `characters.*`, `scholars.*`
**Template:**
```
**[CANDIDATE_NAME]** ([BIRTH]-[DEATH])
Candidate for: **[CHARACTER_NAME]**
Identification strength: [STRENGTH]
Championed by: [SCHOLAR_NAMES]
```

---

### SECTION 2: The Case For
**Purpose:** Present the evidence supporting this identification.
**Word target:** 200-300 words
**Required elements:**
- Biographical facts that match the sonnets
- Specific sonnets that fit (with line citations)
- Historical connections to Shakespeare
**Data source:** `character_candidates.evidence`, linked sonnets from `character_appearances`
**Anti-patterns:** Don't claim certainty. Use "the evidence suggests," "this reading fits," "if we accept this identification."

---

### SECTION 3: The Case Against
**Purpose:** Present counterarguments honestly.
**Word target:** 100-200 words
**Required elements:** At least 2 counterarguments or complications.
**Anti-patterns:** Don't strawman the opposition. If the counterarguments are strong, say so.

---

### SECTION 4: Key Sonnets
**Purpose:** Link to the sonnets most relevant to this candidate.
**Required elements:** List of sonnet numbers with first lines, each with a one-sentence note on why it's relevant to this candidate.
**Data source:** `character_appearances` filtered by character, joined with `sonnets`

## TEMPLATE 4: Thematic Essay

**Format:** Freestanding scholarly essay on a cross-cutting theme
**Audience:** Educated general reader; students; anyone wanting deeper context
**Total target:** 1,500-2,500 words
**Tone:** Essayistic — more discursive than the per-sonnet analysis, more personal voice, but still grounded in evidence.

### SECTION 1: Title + Abstract
**Purpose:** Hook and orient.
**Required elements:** Title (compelling, not generic), subtitle (clarifying), 2-3 sentence abstract.
**Data source:** `essays.title`, `essays.subtitle`, `essays.abstract`
**Anti-patterns:** Don't title essays "About [Topic]." Use a phrase from the sonnets or a provocative formulation.
**Example opening line:** "Shakespeare's Speaker is the most unreliable narrator in English poetry — and he knows it."

---

### SECTION 2: Argument
**Purpose:** The essay's central claim, developed across 3-5 sections.
**Word target:** 1,200-2,000 words
**Required elements:**
- A thesis stated in the first paragraph
- Verbatim citations from at least 5 different sonnets (with line numbers)
- At least 2 scholarly perspectives referenced
- Attention to the FYM/DL dramatic situation (not just abstract themes)
**Data source:** `essays.body_markdown`, `sonnet_analyses.*`, `line_devices.*`, `scholars.*`

---

### SECTION 3: Sonnets Discussed
**Purpose:** Quick-reference list of all sonnets cited, with links.
**Data source:** `essay_sonnet_links` with relevance level

## TEMPLATE 5: Sonnet Context Page

**Format:** Historical and literary context for a specific sonnet
**Audience:** Readers who want to know WHEN and WHY, not just WHAT
**Total target:** 400-700 words
**Tone:** Historical, specific, evidence-grounded

### SECTION 1: Position in the Sequence
**Purpose:** Where does this sonnet sit and what surrounds it?
**Required elements:** Sonnet number, group membership, what comes before and after, narrative arc.
**Data source:** `sonnet_groups.*`, `sonnet_group_members.*`

### SECTION 2: The Fair Young Man / Dark Lady
**Purpose:** What this specific sonnet tells us about the character.
**Required elements:** Which candidate(s) fit, what textual evidence this sonnet provides.
**Data source:** `character_appearances.*`, `character_candidates.*`
**Anti-patterns:** Don't just repeat the general case for each candidate. Focus on what THIS sonnet specifically contributes.

### SECTION 3: Historical Context
**Purpose:** What was happening in Shakespeare's world when this was likely written?
**Required elements:** Approximate date range, relevant biographical facts, patron relationships.
**Data source:** `contexts.*`

### SECTION 4: Literary Context
**Purpose:** How this sonnet relates to the broader tradition.
**Required elements:** Petrarchan convention (if relevant), contemporary sonnet sequences, classical sources.
**Data source:** `sonnet_modes` where mode_id = 'INTERTEXTUAL'

## Cross-Template Anti-Patterns

1. **Never paraphrase when you can quote.** The sonnets are 14 lines — there is no excuse for not quoting the actual words.
2. **Never list rhetorical devices without explaining their dramatic effect.** "This line contains a metaphor" is not analysis. "This metaphor transforms the FYM from lover to debtor, placing the relationship in the language of finance" IS analysis.
3. **Never treat the Speaker as Shakespeare without qualification.** "The Speaker (whether Shakespeare himself or a dramatic persona)..." — always leave the door open.
4. **Never ignore the puns.** Shakespeare's sonnets are built on puns. If a word has two meanings, both are active. Booth's edition is the guide here.
5. **Never forget the dramatic situation.** Every sonnet is a speech by a character in a situation. Who is speaking? To whom? What do they want? What are they afraid of? This is the lens that makes scholarship come alive for the audiobook project.
6. **Never write a generic first sentence.** Every opening line should make the reader want to read the next one.
7. **Always anchor claims in specific lines.** "The sonnet deals with time" is vague. "The sonnet's argument against time pivots at line 9, where 'But' reverses eight lines of nature's deficiency" is anchored.
